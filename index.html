Discover the Ocean's Wonders

Do you know that the Ocean is constituted of 5 main zones? Let's dive!

30-2°C

Sunlit Zone

200 meters deep

The sunlit, known as the Euphotic Zone, is the thinnest ocean zone that measures about 200 meters(656 feet) deep. There, light penetrates the water, which allows photosynthesis to occur. The surface waters are warmerthan deep waters and reach a temperature of 30 Degrees Celcius.

Importance of the zone:

The zone allows picoplankton to live, which serves as food for animals like zooplankton and other fish, contributing to sustaining the food chain cycle. The sunlit zone is where more aquatic ecosystems live. Below are examples of fishes and marine ecosystems that live in that zone.

4-5°C

Twilight Zone

200-1000 meters deep

The twilight zone, also known as the Mesopelagic Zone, measures about 200-1000 meters deep. At this ocean level, creatures are bioluminescent or have huge mouths. As we dive deeper, less sunlight penetrates the waters. The water is cooler than the surface waters and reaches a temperature of 4-5 Degrees Celcius.

Importance of protecting that zone:

This zone helps in regulating the amount of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere. The ocean is good at absorbing carbon dioxide in the atmosphere, which creates balance on the planet. Secondly, marine snow is produced from the absorption of carbon dioxide by Phytoplankton, which is crucial in the ocean food chain. Many marine animals feed on them, ensuring the survival of many marine species and the stability of our planet.

4° C

Midlight Zone

1000- 4000 meters deep

The midnight zone, also known as the Bathypelagic Zone, is the deepest ocean zone, about 1000-4000 meters deep. This zone is full of mysteries as no light penetrates the water there. The ocean at this level is in complete darkness, allowing more strange and bioluminescent aquatic animals to live. The temperature there is so cold, reaching 4 Degrees Celcius.

Importance of the zone:

The midnight zone is home to many larvae that develop in other regions. This zone helps sustain life in the marine ecosystem as zooplankton move down from the twilight zone to protect themselves from predators.The midnight zone is 70% of our waters, allowing carbon dioxide to be absorbed and consumed by the marine ecosystem.

2-3°C

Abyssal Zone

4000-6500 meters deep

The abyssal zone, also known as the abyssopelagic zone, is the coldest ocean zone, hitting a temperature of 2 to 3 degrees Celsius. It is about 4000-6500 meters deep and is an area of complete darkness where light is nonexistent. It is an unexplored area, but scientists say animals communicate using bioluminescence.

Importance of the zone:

This area is important as it is filled with minerals that are often used in manufacturing. Minerals like cobalt, aluminium, and nickel are often extracted from this zone and used in manufacturing car parts and other products. This area also accounts for one-third of the planet's sea floor and has an atmospheric pressure of 600, which makes it difficult to dive into these depths.

1° C

Hadal Zone

6500 - 11000 meters deep

The Hadal Zone, also known as the Hadopelagic Zone, measures about 6500-11,000 meters deep and is one of the coldest places in the ocean, hitting temperatures of 1 degree Celsius and less. The Hadal Zone is often present in areas where there are trenches. Trenches are often caused by tectonic plate motion.

Importance of the zone:

The hadal zone is formed when two tectonic plates are subducted one over the other, which creates trenches. Marine life grows in these trenches, creating habitats for bioluminescent organisms. This zone is very important as it helps regulate climates and absorb carbon dioxide. There is more to discover, and scientists are working hard to understand this zone's benefits and mysteries, which are yet to be discovered.
